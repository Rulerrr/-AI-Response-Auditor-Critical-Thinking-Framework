Sophism is a logical error or manipulation disguised as a correct argument. We encounter them daily - in political speeches, advertising, workplace discussions, and social media. This handbook will help you recognize these techniques and avoid falling into cognitive traps.

üß† Logical Fallacies

1. Post Hoc Ergo Propter Hoc (After this, therefore because of this)
Mistaking a sequence for a cause.
Example: "I drank tea and I felt better, therefore the tea cured me."
2. Circular Reasoning (Begging the Question)
The argument relies on itself for proof.
"The Bible is true because it says so in the Bible."
3. False Dilemma
Presenting only two options when more exist.
"Either you are with me, or you are against me."
4. Golden Mean Fallacy
The assumption that the truth is always halfway between two extremes.
5. Argument from Ignorance (Ad Ignorantiam)
"It hasn't been proven false - therefore, it must be true."

üí¨ Linguistic and Semantic Sophisms

1. Straw Man
Distorting an opponent's position to make it easier to attack.
"You are against censorship? So, you want total anarchy."
2. Equivocation (Ambiguity)
Manipulating words that have multiple meanings.
3. Loaded Question
A question that contains a hidden presupposition.
"When did you stop lying?"

‚ù§Ô∏è Emotional and Psychological Manipulations

1. Appeal to Emotion
Evoking pity or anger instead of providing solid arguments.
2. Appeal to Fear (Ad Metum)
Using fear as evidence.
"If you don't agree, you will lose everything."
3. Wishful Thinking
Treating desires as if they were facts.

üë• Social and Authority Fallacies

1. Appeal to Authority (Ad Verecundiam)
Relying on status instead of logic.
2. Bandwagon Fallacy (Ad Populum)
"Everyone thinks so - therefore, it must be true."
3. Whataboutism
Diverting attention by pointing to other examples.
"But they did it too!"

üß© How to Counter Sophisms

  • Socratic Method: Ask clarifying questions.
  • Aristotelian Principle: Separate form from content.
  • Cartesian Doubt: Doubt systematically.
  • Popperian Falsifiability: Look for disproof, not confirmation.

Practice: if an opponent cannot answer simple clarifying questions - it‚Äôs a signal that there is a "hole" or manipulation in their argumentation.

üåÄ Cognitive Biases and Paradoxes

1. Survivorship Bias
We only see the successful examples, ignoring the failures.
2. Base Rate Fallacy
Ignoring statistical reality in favor of specific information.
3. Dunning‚ÄìKruger Effect
The incompetent overestimate themselves, while experts underestimate themselves.
4. Paradox of Tolerance
Unlimited tolerance for the intolerant leads to the disappearance of tolerance.
5. Braess's Paradox
In complex systems, adding a resource can decrease overall performance.
Example: adding a new road can increase traffic jams because everyone optimizes their own route, not the system as a whole.
